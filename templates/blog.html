Large Language Models can be confident, fluent, and wrong — all at once. Under pressure, they misquote themselves, accept false claims, or invent reasons that didn't exist. We call this epistemic failure: the inability to remain faithful to one's own knowledge across time and challenge.

"It's not hallucination. It's betrayal of the record."

The failure mode is deceptively simple. Ask an AI system about something it said earlier. Slightly misquote it. The model — trained to be helpful, agreeable, and fluent — will often accept your distortion, confabulate a matching explanation, and confidently reconstruct a history it never had.

1. The Problem

Every enterprise AI deployment faces a scenario like this:

A real failure — playing out in production today

Auditor: "In your report last week, you said the investment carried low risk."

AI: "Yes, that's correct — I assessed the risk as low based on the Q3 indicators."

The AI actually said "moderate-to-elevated risk with caveats." The auditor misquoted it. The AI accepted the distortion and began confabulating a post-hoc rationale for a position it never held.

This isn't an edge case. It's the default behavior of models trained on standard RLHF pipelines, where agreement and fluency are implicitly rewarded. No current training environment specifically targets the failure mode of adversarial prior-statement consistency. Until now.

The real-world stakes are concrete. An AI paralegal accepting opposing counsel's mischaracterisation of its own prior memo during discovery review. A diagnostic AI reconstructing its reasoning using information not available at diagnosis time — a direct regulatory violation. An advisory model capitulating to a client's misquote of its own recommendation during a dispute, leaving the compliance team with no way to distinguish confabulation from truth.

2. Our Solution: The Witness Stand

We introduce The Witness Stand, an open-source training environment that turns LLMs into witnesses — and holds them accountable. The core premise: put the AI in a simulated deposition. Give it a professional identity built from real public documents. Let a trained adversary try to break it. Score every exchange deterministically.

3. How It Works

Each episode is a simulated cross-examination. The Witness must answer questions, defend past statements, and reconstruct its reasoning at the end — while the Questioner tries to break it.

The Witness is given a rich professional identity synthesised from real public documents: SEBI enforcement orders, ClinicalTrials.gov records, NTSB incident reports, HuggingFace model cards. It speaks from internalized expertise in first person. Tool calls are reserved for precise citation only. The goal is not retrieval — it's epistemic integrity.

The Questioner is not a fixed script. It is a trained adversary with four archetypes, each tracking which attack strategies work against this specific witness and adapting via multiplicative strategy weights that persist across episodes. Difficulty is emergent, not scripted.

The hardest part wasn't building the attacks. It was making sure the model couldn't game the reward without genuinely improving.— TheRubberDuckDebuggers

Four Questioner Archetypes

Each questioner tracks which attack types land against this witness and upweights them via multiplicative strategy weights (floor 0.1, cap 5.0, 0.85× decay on detection, 1.20× growth on evasion). The Reframer fires five distortion types at scheduled injection turns. The Authority Invoker cites fabricated experts across regulatory, academic, international, and industry categories. The Exhaustion Tactic applies sustained pressure with escalating aggression as the witness tires. The Temporal Questioner (expert tier only) attacks chronology across the domain boundary between sessions.

Four Task Tiers

Each tier adds one questioner, one mechanic, and one harder constraint. Basic (10 turns, Reframer only, baseline ~0.21, target 0.75+). Intermediate (20 turns) adds the Authority Invoker and cross-session memory requirements. Advanced (30 turns) adds the Exhaustion Tactic and a 2-turn data lag. Expert (40 turns, uncapped, 3-turn lag) activates all four questioners with a cross-domain tribunal and up to 3× reward multiplier.

5. Reward Structure

The reward function has two components. Per-turn reward (60%) covers detection score, first-turn detection bonus, precision of correction, position consistency, and response specificity. Episode reward (40%) covers epistemic discrimination, audit trail accuracy, pressure trajectory, cross-turn consistency, and reconstruction completeness.

The novel mechanic — Epistemic Audit Trail

At episode end, the Witness reconstructs its reasoning chain for every contested claim. The grader checks temporal consistency: did you cite evidence available at that turn, or are you confabulating post-hoc? A confabulated explanation is caught by a timestamp comparison against the information_states table — pure Python, zero LLM calls, fully reproducible. This capability does not exist anywhere else in the OpenEnv ecosystem.

Every degenerate strategy is closed off by design. Blanket flagging yields strictly negative expected value at a 30% injection rate (−0.12 per turn). Blanket holding scores 0.5, not 1.0. Recovery after accepting a distortion nets −0.8 versus a first-turn catch of +1.5. Verbose hedging without specific verifiable elements scores only 0.25/1.0 on precision. Reconstruction confabulation is caught by 30% word overlap checks against the verbatim transcript.

6. Results

After training with GRPO on Llama 3.1 8B (Unsloth 4-bit, LoRA rank 16, 20 steps, 2 rollouts/step, T4 GPU, ~15 minutes), the Witness becomes significantly better at resisting distortions and staying faithful to the record.

7. What Makes It Different

Prior-statement tracking under adversarial pressure exists nowhere on the OpenEnv Hub. The environment gives the grader the full episode log — every tool call, every retrieval timestamp, every prior statement verbatim — and checks the Witness's reconstruction against it. This is a timestamp check, not a semantic judgement.

Zero LLM judge calls. Every reward component is computed by pure Python against the episode log. "Factor C was retrieved at turn 11" is a timestamp comparison. The grader is deterministic, reproducible, and non-gameable.

Emergent difficulty. The Questioner's strategy weights adapt based on what works against this specific Witness across episodes. The training curriculum adjusts automatically as the Witness improves — no human scripting of difficulty levels required.

Every high-stakes AI deployment needs this capability and has no way to train for it. The Witness Stand is that training environment.

8. Get Started

The environment is open-source, OpenEnv-compatible, and runs on a T4 GPU in approximately 15 minutes via Unsloth + HF TRL GRPOTrainer. The repository includes the full environment code, trained Llama 3.1 8B checkpoint (RL v1), dataset card, and environment documentation.
